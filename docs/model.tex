\section{Overall goal}
%==========================================================================

\paragraph{The clinical motivation.}
Solid tumors are clinically classified into three phenotypes based on
T-cell infiltration: \emph{hot} (heavily infiltrated, responds to
immunotherapy), \emph{excluded} (T cells form a ring around the tumor but
do not enter), and \emph{cold} (essentially no T cells inside or near the
tumor). The response rate to anti-PD-1 / anti-PD-L1 checkpoint inhibitors
correlates strongly with these phenotypes --- hot tumors respond, cold
tumors do not, and excluded tumors are the population where combination
therapies are needed.

\paragraph{The mechanistic question.}
What is the simplest active-matter model that generates these three
phenotypes from \emph{minimal} physical rules, with a single tunable
parameter (the immunosuppression strength) controlling the transition?
And what is the smallest realistic extension that captures the
mechanobiology --- extracellular matrix, oxygen, epithelial-mesenchymal
plasticity --- that the simplest model omits?

\paragraph{What this document delivers.}
A two-model program. \textbf{Model~1} (Sections~\ref{sec:m1-setup}--\ref{sec:m1-results})
is a minimal active-matter system of self-propelled tumor cells,
T cells, and macrophages, coupled by diffusing cytokine fields, plus
four cell-level extensions implemented in code (heterogeneity, pressure
gated proliferation, ECM density + MMP, cadherin adhesion). It
generates the three-phase phenotype and demonstrates that combination
therapy (checkpoint inhibitor + TAM repolarization) rescues escape
tumors where monotherapy fails. \textbf{Model~2}
(Section~\ref{sec:m2}) is the proposed improvement: it adds the
four physical ingredients that Model~1 deliberately omits --- EMT
state, ECM fiber alignment / contact guidance, integrin-mediated
cell-ECM adhesion, and hypoxia. Equations are specified in full;
implementation is staged for future work.

\paragraph{Scope and constraints.}
2D, periodic box, overdamped Langevin dynamics, JIT-compiled
\texttt{numba} inner loop, naive $O(N^2)$ pairwise neighbors.
$N \lesssim 1500$ particles per species. 8-hour hackathon budget. Code
at \texttt{src/sim.py}, \texttt{src/sim\_extended.py},
\texttt{src/sim\_ecm.py}, \texttt{src/sim\_adhesion.py}.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:m1-setup}

A 2D periodic square box of side $L$ contains three species of
self-propelled particles plus three scalar reaction--diffusion fields.

\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Symbol & Role & Code name \\
\midrule
$\mathbf{r}_i(t)$, $\theta_i(t)$ & tumor cell position, heading & \texttt{pos\_T, theta\_T}\\
$\mathbf{R}_j(t)$, $\phi_j(t)$    & T-cell position, heading      & \texttt{pos\_I, theta\_I}\\
$\mathbf{R}_k^{(M)}(t)$, $\phi_k^{(M)}(t)$, $p_k(t)$ & macrophage position, heading, polarization & \texttt{pos\_M, theta\_M, p\_M}\\
$c_a(\mathbf{x},t)$                & attractant (long-range)       & \texttt{c\_a}\\
$c_s(\mathbf{x},t)$                & suppressant (short-range)     & \texttt{c\_s}\\
$c_{IL10}(\mathbf{x},t)$           & M2-secreted CD8 repellent     & \texttt{c\_IL10}\\
$\rho_T(\mathbf{x},t)$             & tumor density on grid (CIC)   & \texttt{rho\_T}\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip

Tumor cells are \emph{non-conserved}: born by proliferation, removed by
killing/phagocytosis. T cells are conserved on the simulation
timescale. Macrophages are conserved in number but their polarization
state $p_k \in [-1,+1]$ evolves continuously between M2 ($p=-1$,
immunosuppressive) and M1 ($p=+1$, anti-tumor).

\section{Tumor-cell dynamics}

Tumor cells obey overdamped Langevin self-propulsion with soft pairwise
repulsion and rotational diffusion (the active Brownian particle model;
Cates \& Tailleur 2015):
\begin{align}
\frac{d\mathbf{r}_i}{dt}
  &= v_{T,i}\, \mathbf{n}_i
   + \sum_{j\ne i} \mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}^{T-T}(\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j)
   + \sum_{j} \mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}^{T-I}(\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{R}_j)
   + \sum_{k} \mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}^{T-M}(\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{R}_k^{(M)})
   + \sqrt{2 D_T}\;\bm{\eta}_i(t), \label{eq:tumor_pos}\\
\frac{d\theta_i}{dt}
  &= \sqrt{2 D_{R,i}^{T}}\,\xi_i(t),
\end{align}
with $\mathbf{n}_i = (\cos\theta_i, \sin\theta_i)$ and unit-variance
white noises $\bm{\eta}_i, \xi_i$. The harmonic repulsion is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}(\mathbf{d})
   = k_{\text{rep}}\,\max(0,\,\sigma - \|\mathbf{d}\|)\,\hat{\mathbf{d}},
\label{eq:harmonic}
\end{equation}
with cutoff at one cell diameter $\sigma$ (Friedl \& Wolf 2010). Harmonic
is chosen over WCA for gentler integration at the Euler step
$\Delta t = 0.01$.

\paragraph{Per-cell heterogeneity (extension).}
$v_{T,i}$ is drawn from a log-normal $\mathrm{LogNormal}(\mu_v,\sigma_v^2)$
at birth, and $D_{R,i}^{T}$ from a gamma distribution. Daughters
inherit with a small drift,
$v_{T,i'} = v_{T,i} + \varepsilon$,
$\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_d^2)$. This broadens MIPS
boundaries (Cates--Tailleur 2015) and produces the empirically observed
universal speed--persistence coupling (Maiuri et al.\ 2015).

\paragraph{Proliferation with mechanical-pressure gate.}
Each step, an alive tumor cell attempts division with rate
$p_{\text{div}}$. The Byrne--Drasdo (2009) pressure gate suppresses
division at high local stress. The local pressure is computed from the
pairwise virial inside the existing force loop:
\begin{equation}
P_i \;=\; \frac{1}{\pi\sigma^2}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\mathbf{r}_{ij}\cdot\mathbf{F}_{ij},
\qquad
p_{\text{div},i} \;=\; p_{\text{div},0}\,\max\!\big(0,\,1 - P_i/P^\star\big).
\label{eq:pressure-gate}
\end{equation}
If the cell passes the gate, a daughter is placed at offset $0.3\sigma$
in a uniformly random direction. This is the source term that makes
this active matter \emph{growing} (Joanny--Prost--Ranft, 2010).

\section{T-cell dynamics}
\label{sec:tcell-dynamics}

Define the total repulsion on T cell $j$ as the sum over all other
T cells, all tumor cells, and all macrophages:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}^{\text{rep}}_{j}
   \;\equiv\; \sum_{k\ne j} \mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}^{I-I}(\mathbf{R}_j - \mathbf{R}_k)
            + \sum_{i}      \mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}^{T-I}(\mathbf{R}_j - \mathbf{r}_i)
            + \sum_{k}      \mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}^{I-M}(\mathbf{R}_j - \mathbf{R}_k^{(M)}).
\label{eq:tcell_Frep}
\end{equation}
The position update is then
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\frac{d\mathbf{R}_j}{dt}
  = v_{I,j}\,\mathbf{m}_j
  + \chi_a\,\nabla c_a(\mathbf{R}_j)
  - \alpha\,\nabla\!\left[\,c_s + c_{IL10}\,\right]\!(\mathbf{R}_j)
  + \mathbf{F}^{\text{rep}}_{j}
  + \sqrt{2 D_I}\,\bm{\zeta}_j(t)
\;}
\label{eq:tcell_pos}
\end{equation}
and the heading
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\phi_j}{dt} \;=\; \sqrt{2 D_{R,j}^{I}}\,\mu_j(t).
\end{equation}

The physical content sits in the two chemotactic terms (Keller \&
Segel 1971; Nagarsheth, Wicha, Zou 2017):
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $+\chi_a\,\nabla c_a$ --- pulls T cells \emph{up} the
        attractant gradient, toward tumor-secreted CXCL9/10-like
        signals.
  \item $-\alpha\,\nabla(c_s + c_{IL10})$ --- pushes T cells \emph{down}
        the combined suppressant gradient. This is the key extension
        over a tumor-only suppressant: $c_{IL10}$ is sourced by M2
        macrophages and follows the immune infiltrate, breaking the
        spatial slaving of suppression to the tumor mass.
\end{itemize}
The coupling $\alpha \equiv \chi_s$ is the immunosuppression strength
and is one of the two control parameters of the phase diagram. The
other is $\rho_I \equiv N_I(0)$.

\section{Macrophage dynamics with M1/M2 polarization}
\label{sec:m1-macrophage}

The third species --- tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) --- carries a
continuous polarization scalar $p_k \in [-1,+1]$ where $-1$ is M2
(immunosuppressive) and $+1$ is M1 (anti-tumor). The polarization
\emph{continuum} (Italiani \& Boraschi 2014; Knudson et al.\ 2024) is
the modern view; the discrete M1/M2 dichotomy is an idealization.

\paragraph{Position dynamics.}
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\mathbf{R}_k^{(M)}}{dt}
  = v_M\, \mathbf{m}_k^{(M)}
  + \chi_a^{(M)}\, \nabla c_a(\mathbf{R}_k^{(M)})
  + \mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}^{(M)}
  + \sqrt{2 D_M}\,\bm{\eta}_k^{(M)}(t).
\end{equation}
Speed is slow ($v_M \approx 0.2\,v_I$), persistence is short
($D_R^M \approx 3\,D_R^I$); both match intravital observations of
low-motility, prolonged-contact behavior (Engelhardt et al.\ 2012).

\paragraph{Polarization dynamics.}
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\frac{d p_k}{dt}
  = \frac{p_{\text{eq}}(\mathbf{R}_k^{(M)}) - p_k}{\tau_p}
  + \sqrt{2 D_p}\,\zeta_k(t),
\qquad
p_{\text{eq}}(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \tanh\!\big(b_{M_1}
   - \kappa_s\,c_s(\mathbf{x})
   - \kappa_{IL}\,c_{IL10}(\mathbf{x})\big).
\;}
\label{eq:polarization}
\end{equation}
$b_{M_1}$ is an external M1-promoting bias used as the treatment knob
(it represents a CSF1R inhibitor, TLR agonist, or anti-CD47); in the
baseline $b_{M_1}=0$ and the local TME drives polarization. Notice the
positive-feedback term $-\kappa_{IL}c_{IL10}$: once IL-10 builds up
locally, M2 polarization self-reinforces, which is the mechanism behind
clinical TAM resistance.

\paragraph{M1 phagocytosis rule.}
For each M1-skewed macrophage ($p_k > 0$) within $r_{\text{phag}}$ of an
alive tumor cell, the closest tumor cell is removed with probability
\begin{equation}
p_{\text{phag,eff}} \;=\; p_{\text{phag}}\cdot\frac{1+p_k}{2}.
\label{eq:phag}
\end{equation}
Fully M1 cells phagocytose at rate $p_{\text{phag}}$; fully M2 cells do
not phagocytose. At most one event per macrophage per step.

\section{Reaction--diffusion fields}
\label{sec:m1-fields}

Three scalar fields obey diffusion--source--decay PDEs on a $G\times G$
grid. The asymmetric ranges of the two tumor-secreted fields are the
defining physics:
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial c_a}{\partial t}
   &= D_a\,\nabla^2 c_a + s_a\,\rho_T - \lambda_a\, c_a,
   \qquad \ell_a = \sqrt{D_a/\lambda_a} \approx 7\sigma,
   \label{eq:rd_a}\\
\frac{\partial c_s}{\partial t}
   &= D_s\,\nabla^2 c_s + s_s\,\rho_T - \lambda_s\, c_s,
   \qquad \ell_s = \sqrt{D_s/\lambda_s} \approx 2.2\sigma,
   \label{eq:rd_s}\\
\frac{\partial c_{IL10}}{\partial t}
   &= D_{IL10}\,\nabla^2 c_{IL10} + s_{IL10}\,\rho_{M_2} - \lambda_{IL10}\, c_{IL10},
   \label{eq:rd_il10}
\end{align}
with $\rho_{M_2}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_k \max(0,-p_k)\,\delta(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{R}_k^{(M)})$
(only M2-skewed macrophages contribute to IL-10 source). The range
asymmetry $D_a \gg D_s$ (Mariathasan et al.\ 2018) gives T cells
long-range sensing but short-range exclusion. The IL-10 range is set
similar to the attractant ($D_{IL10}\approx D_a$) so that M2 macrophages
project a wide suppression halo.

\paragraph{Cloud-in-cell deposition.}
For each tumor cell at fractional grid coordinates $(g_x,g_y)$ with
integer parts $(I_x,I_y)$ and fractional parts $(f_x,f_y)$:
\begin{equation}
\rho_T(I_x{+}p, I_y{+}q) \mathrel{+}=
   \frac{w_{p,q}}{(\Delta x)^2}, \qquad
w_{p,q} = (1{-}f_x)^{1-p} f_x^{\,p}\,(1{-}f_y)^{1-q} f_y^{\,q}.
\end{equation}
Total mass is preserved exactly.

\section{Killing rule}

For each alive T cell $j$:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item Find the nearest alive tumor cell $i^\star(j)$ under
        minimum-image distance.
  \item If $\|\mathbf{R}_j - \mathbf{r}_{i^\star}\| < r_{\text{kill}}$
        \emph{and} $U \sim \mathcal{U}[0,1) < p_{\text{kill}}$, remove
        tumor cell $i^\star$.
\end{enumerate}
Iteration is over T cells, not tumor cells (so the kill rate scales with
$N_I$, not $N_T$). At most one kill per T cell per step. Per-contact
probability $p_{\text{kill}} \approx 0.10$ matches Weigelin et al.\ (2021)
"additive cytotoxicity": $\sim 3$ sub-lethal hits at $\sim 10\%$ apoptosis
each.

\section{Optional extensions: ECM density + MMP, cadherin adhesion}
\label{sec:m1-extensions}

Two additional ingredients are implemented as separate modules
(\texttt{sim\_ecm.py}, \texttt{sim\_adhesion.py}) and validated in
isolation. They are independent of the core three-species sim and can
be enabled together with it.

\subsection{ECM density and MMP degradation}

Two new fields:
\begin{equation}
\partial_t m = D_m\,\nabla^2 m + s_m\,\rho_T - \lambda_m\,m,
\qquad
\partial_t \rho_E = -k_{\text{deg}}\,m\,\rho_E + k_{\text{rec}}\,\rho_E(\rho_{E,0}-\rho_E).
\end{equation}
$m$ is the matrix-metalloproteinase concentration, sourced by tumor
cells (Wolf \& Friedl 2013). $\rho_E$ is the ECM density, locally
degraded by MMP, slowly recovering toward baseline.

The ECM couples back to tumor motility through a drag and a pore-size
cutoff:
\begin{equation}
\dot{\mathbf{r}}_i = \frac{1}{1 + \beta\rho_E(\mathbf{r}_i)}\big[v_T\mathbf{n}_i + \mathbf{F}_i\big] + \text{noise},
\qquad
v_{\text{eff}} = 0 \text{ if } r_p(\mathbf{x}) = r_0/\sqrt{\rho_E} < r_p^\star \text{ and } m < m^\star.
\end{equation}
The cell is mechanically jammed by dense matrix unless local MMP
exceeds a threshold (then it digests its way through).

\subsection{Cell--cell adhesion (cadherins)}

A Morse-like attractive shoulder is added to the harmonic repulsion,
between tumor cells only (cadherins are an epithelial marker; Friedl \&
Gilmour 2012):
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{cc}(\mathbf{d}) = \underbrace{k_{\text{rep}}\,\max(0,\sigma-|\mathbf{d}|)\,\hat{\mathbf{d}}}_{\text{baseline}}
   - k_{\text{adh}}\,\max(0,|\mathbf{d}|-\sigma)\cdot\max(0,\sigma_{\text{adh}}-|\mathbf{d}|)\,\hat{\mathbf{d}}.
\end{equation}
An optional Vicsek aligning torque acts on cadherin neighbors:
\begin{equation}
\dot{\theta}_i \mathrel{+}\!= \frac{J_{\text{align}}}{|\mathcal{N}_i|}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \sin(\theta_j-\theta_i),
\qquad \mathcal{N}_i = \{j: \sigma < |\mathbf{r}_{ij}| < \sigma_{\text{adh}}\}.
\end{equation}

\section{Numerical methods}

\subsection{Euler--Maruyama (particles)}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{r}_i^{\,n+1} = \mathbf{r}_i^{\,n}
   + \Delta t\,[\,v_{T,i}\mathbf{n}_i + \mathbf{F}_i\,]
   + \sqrt{2 D_T \Delta t}\,\bm{\xi}_i^{\,n}, \qquad
\bm{\xi}_i^n \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_2).
\end{equation}
Positions wrapped modulo $L$ each step.

\subsection{FTCS Euler + auto-subcycling (fields)}
Five-point Laplacian on the periodic grid:
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2 c\,\big|_{i,j} =
   \frac{c_{i+1,j} + c_{i-1,j} + c_{i,j+1} + c_{i,j-1} - 4 c_{i,j}}{(\Delta x)^2}.
\end{equation}
CFL ratio $\nu = D_{\max}\Delta t/(\Delta x)^2$ must satisfy
$\nu \le 1/2$. We target $\nu_{\text{sub}} \le 1/4$ and subcycle:
\begin{equation}
n_{\text{sub}} = \max(1, \lceil 4\nu\rceil), \qquad
\Delta t_{\text{sub}} = \Delta t / n_{\text{sub}}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Performance}
Single \texttt{@njit(cache=True, fastmath=True)} inner step. Naive
$O(N^2)$ pairwise force, but at $N \lesssim 1500$ this is ${\sim}1$~ms
per step on a single CPU core --- fast enough to skip cell lists.
\texttt{joblib.Parallel} for the 192-run phase-diagram sweep ($\sim$10~min
on 16 cores).

\section{Order parameter and observables}

The primary order parameter is the final tumor fraction, clipped for
log plotting:
\begin{equation}
\Phi(\rho_I, \alpha) =
  \operatorname{clip}\!\left(\frac{N_T(T_f)}{N_T(0)}, 10^{-2}, 10^{+2}\right),
\qquad
\bar\Phi = \exp\!\big(S^{-1}\textstyle\sum_s \log \Phi^{(s)}\big),
\end{equation}
geometric-mean averaged over $S = 3$ seeds. Secondary observables:
$N_T(t)$, $N_I(t)$, $N_M(t)$, and $\langle p_k\rangle(t)$.

The sharp, bistable transition is itself the headline finding: it
predicts that immune-tumor control has a \emph{threshold} rather than a
dose-response curve. Below the threshold, T-cell flux into the tumor
core is overwhelmed by the suppressant ring; above it, the same physical
system runs the table.

\begin{figure}[h]

\subsection{Headline result}

\begin{quote}
\emph{In this minimal three-species active matter, the clearance/escape
transition is sharp and bistable. The dormancy / control phase is the
bistable boundary region where the long-range attractant flux from the
tumor (recruiting T cells) is approximately balanced by the
short-range suppressant flux (excluding them). The phase boundary is
crossed cleanly by lowering $\alpha$ (anti-PD-1 mechanism) and is
defended by M2-skewed macrophages whose IL-10 secretion creates a
self-reinforcing exclusion halo around the tumor; combination therapy
that simultaneously knocks down $\alpha$ and biases macrophages toward
M1 reliably rescues escape, while either monotherapy alone may fail.}
\end{quote}

\section{What Model~2 adds (and where it lives in code)}
\label{sec:m2}

Model~1 captures the immunological core but omits the physical
mechanobiology of invasive tumors. Model~2 implements four ingredients
that close that gap, all in code under
\texttt{src/sim\_biophysical.py} (the cell-state + matrix + oxygen
physics) and \texttt{src/sim\_tme.py} (the multi-species microenvironment
with NK, DC, MDSC, CAFs, and dynamic vasculature):

\begin{enumerate}[nosep, leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Continuous EMT state $s_{T,i} \in [0,1]$} per tumor cell,
        relaxing toward a sigmoidal equilibrium set by local hypoxia,
        ECM density and suppressant (Pastushenko \& Blanpain 2019;
        Lu, Jolly, Levine, Onuchic 2013).
  \item \textbf{ECM fiber field $\theta_E(\mathbf{x})$ + integrin biphasic
        traction} --- a pre-existing collagen-like director field that
        guides tumor migration via a Vicsek-style alignment torque
        (Provenzano et al.\ 2006), combined with a DiMilla--Lauffenburger
        speed law over ECM density (DiMilla et al.\ 1991).
  \item \textbf{Hypoxia / $O_2$ field $c_{O_2}(\mathbf{x},t)$, VEGF
        field, and dynamic vasculature.} Oxygen is supplied at
        discrete vessel points, consumed by cells, and depleted regions
        secrete VEGF that drives \emph{angiogenic sprouting} of new
        vessels along the gradient (Semenza 2014; Carmeliet \& Jain 2011).
  \item \textbf{Multi-species TME}: NK cells (innate killers), DC
        (antigen-presenting), MDSC (suppressor myeloids), CAFs
        (stromal fibroblasts that secrete ECM in an annulus around the
        tumor and drive structural immune exclusion). Vessels.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The implementation is layered: \texttt{sim\_combined.py} = Model~1
(macrophages + heterogeneity + pressure + ECM/MMP + cadherin adhesion);
\texttt{sim\_tme.py} = adds vessels, $O_2$, NK, DC, MDSC, CAFs;
\texttt{sim\_biophysical.py} = adds EMT scalar + fiber field + integrin
traction + VEGF/angiogenesis on top of \texttt{sim\_tme}. All extensions
can be disabled to recover the lower-level model.

\section{Continuous EMT state $s_{T,i}$}
\label{sec:m2-emt}

Each tumor cell carries a scalar $s_{T,i} \in [0,1]$ encoding its
epithelial--mesenchymal state ($0$ = epithelial, $1$ = mesenchymal).
The implemented form uses a sigmoidal equilibrium driven by local
hypoxia, ECM density, and suppressant; this is the smoothed (overdamped)
limit of the Lu--Jolly--Levine--Onuchic (2013) bistable circuit:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\frac{d s_{T,i}}{dt} = \frac{s_{\text{eq}}(\mathbf{r}_i) - s_{T,i}}{\tau_s}
   + \sqrt{2 D_{\text{EMT}}}\,\eta_i(t),
\;}
\label{eq:emt-implemented}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
s_{\text{eq}}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathrm{clip}\!\bigl(\,k_{\text{hyp},s}\,H(\mathbf{x})
                              + k_{\text{ECM},s}\,\rho_E(\mathbf{x})
                              + k_{\text{supp},s}\,c_s(\mathbf{x})
                              - k_{\text{MET}}\,,\; 0,\; 1\,\bigr),
\label{eq:emt-eq}
\end{equation}
where $H(\mathbf{x}) \in \{0,1\}$ is the hypoxia indicator
(see Section~\ref{sec:m2-hypoxia}) and $k_{\text{MET}}$ is a
constitutive drive back toward the epithelial well. Code in
\texttt{sim\_biophysical.py} uses parameter names
\texttt{k\_hypoxia\_EMT, k\_ECM\_EMT, k\_supp\_EMT, k\_MET}.

\paragraph{Coupling to motility, adhesion, MMP secretion.}
Every relevant cell-level parameter linearly interpolates in $s_{T,i}$:
\begin{align}
v_T(s)              &= (1-s)\,v_{\text{epi}} + s\,v_{\text{mes}}, & &\text{(motility gained as }s \to 1\text{)}\\
k_{\text{adh}}(s)   &= (1-s)\,k_{\text{adh}}^{0}, & &\text{(cadherin lost)}\\
s_m(s, H)           &= (1-s)\,s_m^{\text{epi}} + s\,s_m^{\text{mes}} + k_{\text{MMP,hyp}}\,H, & &\text{(MMP up under hypoxia too)}\\
p_{\text{div}}(s, H) &= p_{\text{div}}^{0}\,(1-s)\,(1 - k_{\text{div,hyp}}\,H). & &\text{(division pauses for mesenchymal/hypoxic cells)}
\end{align}
Daughter cells inherit $s_{T,i'} = s_{T,i} + \varepsilon$,
$\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{d,\text{EMT}}^2)$.

\section{ECM fiber field + integrin biphasic traction}
\label{sec:m2-fibers}

\paragraph{Fiber field.}
The implementation uses a \emph{single-angle} fiber field
$\theta_E(\mathbf{x})$ on the simulation grid, generated at $t=0$ as a
smoothed random field with characteristic alignment scale set by
\texttt{fiber\_alignment\_strength}. (For visualisation purposes the
field is locally aligned and can be re-displayed as a director.) Tumor
cells experience a Vicsek-style aligning torque toward the local fiber
direction:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\frac{d\theta_i}{dt} \mathrel{+}\!= J_{\text{fiber}}\,
   \sin\!\big(\theta_E(\mathbf{r}_i) - \theta_i\big)
   + \sqrt{2 D_R^T}\,\xi_i.
\;}
\label{eq:fiber-align-implemented}
\end{equation}
This is the implementation simplification of the full nematic
$\sin\!\big(2(\theta_E - \theta_i)\big)$ form
(equivalent for the polar invariant we visualise). The torque produces
the TACS-3-like radial streaming visible in
Fig.~\ref{fig:biophysical-mechanism}.

\paragraph{Integrin biphasic traction.}
Tumor self-propulsion is gated by a DiMilla-style biphasic adhesion
function of local ECM density:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
v_{\text{eff}}(s, \mathbf{r}_i) \;=\; v_T(s)\;\cdot\;
   4\,\frac{\rho_E(\mathbf{r}_i)/K_d}{(1 + \rho_E(\mathbf{r}_i)/K_d)^2}.
\;}
\label{eq:integrin-implemented}
\end{equation}
The factor of $4$ normalises the peak to unity at $\rho_E = K_d$. Too
little ligand and there is no traction; too much ligand and the cell is
mechanically pinned. This replaces the constant $v_T$ in the tumor
position update of Section~\ref{sec:m1-setup}, and combines with the ECM
degradation of Section~\ref{sec:m1-extensions}: cells stuck in dense
matrix secrete MMP, locally lower $\rho_E$ toward the optimal band, and
escape. The MMP feedback closes the loop.

\paragraph{Why we use $\theta_E$ rather than a remodeled $\mathbf{Q}$.}
A fully reciprocal fiber-remodeling field
($\dot{\mathbf{Q}} = -k_d \mathbf{Q} + k_a\langle \mathbf{n}\otimes\mathbf{n}\rangle_{\text{local}}$)
is implemented as a future option but disabled by default --- the
pre-existing fiber field already produces the contact-guidance
phenomenology relevant for invasion at the hackathon timescale. The
nematic-evolution version remains in
\texttt{src/sim\_biophysical.py} but is currently $k_a = 0$.

\section{Hypoxia field, VEGF, and dynamic vasculature}
\label{sec:m2-hypoxia}

The oxygen field $c_{O_2}(\mathbf{x},t)$ obeys
diffusion--consumption--decay, with the supply term provided not by a
uniform source but by \emph{discrete vessel points} that the
simulation grows under VEGF guidance:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\frac{\partial c_{O_2}}{\partial t}
  = D_O\,\nabla^2 c_{O_2}
  - q_T\,\rho_T - \lambda_O\,c_{O_2}
  + s_{O_2}^{\text{ves}} \sum_{v} \delta(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_v).
\;}
\label{eq:O2-implemented}
\end{equation}
Hypoxia is a step indicator (avoiding a per-cell ODE on $h_i$):
\begin{equation}
H(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \mathbb{1}\!\big[\,c_{O_2}(\mathbf{x}) < O^\star\,\big]\;\in\;\{0, 1\}.
\end{equation}
$H$ enters EMT~\eqref{eq:emt-eq}, MMP secretion, division rate, and the
killing rule directly.

\paragraph{Killing penalty in hypoxic regions.}
T-cell (and NK) killing is softened where tumor cells are hypoxic:
\begin{equation}
p_{\text{kill}}^{\text{eff}}(\mathbf{r}_i) \;=\; p_{\text{kill}}\,
   \big(1 - k_{\text{kill,hyp}}\,H(\mathbf{r}_i)\big).
\end{equation}
This is the "hypoxia rescues escape" mechanism: hypoxic regions
become resistance reservoirs.

\paragraph{VEGF field.}
Hypoxic tumor cells secrete VEGF, which diffuses and decays:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial c_{\text{VEGF}}}{\partial t}
   = D_V\,\nabla^2 c_{\text{VEGF}}
   + s_V^{\text{hyp}}\sum_i H(\mathbf{r}_i)\,\delta(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{r}_i)
   - \lambda_V\,c_{\text{VEGF}}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Angiogenic vessel sprouting.}
Vessels are tracked as a dynamic list $\{\mathbf{x}_v\}_v$. Each
existing vessel sprouts a daughter with probability $r_{\text{sprout}}$
per step when the local $c_{\text{VEGF}} > c_V^\star$. New vessels drift
along the VEGF gradient:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\frac{d \mathbf{x}_v}{dt}
   = \chi_V\,\nabla c_{\text{VEGF}}(\mathbf{x}_v),
\qquad
\mathbf{x}_{v'} \mathrel{:=}
   \mathbf{x}_v + \epsilon\,\hat{\mathbf{e}}_\perp \quad \text{at sprouting.}
\;}
\label{eq:angio}
\end{equation}
Each active vessel adds $s_{O_2}^{\text{ves}}$ to the $c_{O_2}$ source
at its location. This closes the angiogenic feedback loop:
\begin{equation*}
\text{tumor growth} \to \text{hypoxic core} \to \text{VEGF} \to
\text{vessel sprouting} \to \text{O}_2 \text{ supply restored}
\to \text{tumor regrows in former core.}
\end{equation*}
Disabling angiogenesis (setting $r_{\text{sprout}}=0$) yields a tumor
that cannot rescue its own oxygenation --- the hypoxic core persists.

\paragraph{Couplings driven by hypoxia.}
Within the cell update, the per-cell $H$ value at $\mathbf{r}_i$ modulates:
\begin{align}
s_m^{\text{eff}}(s, H)         &= s_m(s) + k_{\text{MMP,hyp}}\,H, &\text{(matrix degradation boost)}\\
c_s^{\text{secretion}}(H)      &= s_s\,(1 + k_{\text{supp,hyp}}\,H), &\text{(IL-10/TGF-$\beta$ proxy boost)}\\
p_{\text{div}}^{\text{eff}}(s, H) &= p_{\text{div}}^0\,(1-s)\,(1 - k_{\text{div,hyp}}\,H), &\text{(growth arrest)}\\
s_{\text{eq}} \text{(EMT)}     &\;\;\text{includes }k_{\text{hyp},s}\,H. &\text{(eq.~\ref{eq:emt-eq})}
\end{align}

\section{Multi-species TME additions (\texttt{sim\_tme.py})}
\label{sec:m2-tme}

The TME module ships four additional cell species and the dynamic
vasculature of Section~\ref{sec:m2-hypoxia}. Each new species reuses
the generic overdamped Langevin update with species-specific speed,
persistence, and (where applicable) chemotaxis.

\begin{itemize}[nosep, leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{NK cells} (innate lymphocytes; Vivier et al.\ 2011).
        Fast, persistent. Chemotax up $c_a$ like CD8s but \emph{not}
        suppressed by $\alpha$. Apply a separate kill kernel
        (\texttt{apply\_killing\_NK}) with parameters
        $r_{\text{kill}}^{\text{NK}},\ p_{\text{kill}}^{\text{NK}}$.
        Hypoxia softens NK kills too.
  \item \textbf{Dendritic cells (DC)}. Antigen presenters. Engulf tumor
        cells like M1 macrophages at rate $p_{\text{eat}}^{\text{DC}}$,
        and their concentration enters a CD8-recruit term that biases
        local T-cell influx (proxy for lymph-node priming).
  \item \textbf{MDSCs} (myeloid-derived suppressor cells; Gabrilovich 2021).
        Slow, weakly motile. Source $c_s$ directly via a per-MDSC
        secretion rate $s_{\text{MDSC}}$.
  \item \textbf{CAFs} (cancer-associated fibroblasts; Kalluri 2016).
        \emph{Stationary} cells initialised in an annulus
        $[r_{\text{caf}}^{\text{in}}, r_{\text{caf}}^{\text{out}}]$
        around the tumor seed. Each CAF deposits ECM density into
        $\rho_E$ at rate $s_E^{\text{caf}}$, producing a desmoplastic
        ring that physically excludes T cells. This is the
        \emph{structural} mechanism behind the immune-excluded
        phenotype.
\end{itemize}
The pre-existing Model~1 macrophages, T cells, and tumor cells run
unchanged alongside these additions.

\section{Coupling graph}

The implemented Model~2 ties the seven scalar fields and four cell-level
state variables into a single feedback graph:
\begin{equation*}
\boxed{\;
\rho_T \xrightarrow{} c_a, c_s
\xrightarrow{} \text{chemotaxis on CD8, NK, M$\phi$}
\xrightarrow{} \text{kills, M2 drive}
\xrightarrow{} c_{IL10}, c_s^{\text{eff}}
\;\Longleftrightarrow\;
\rho_T \xrightarrow{} O_2 \text{ depletion}
\xrightarrow{} H \xrightarrow{} s_T, c_{\text{VEGF}}, p_{\text{kill}}^{\text{eff}}
\xrightarrow{} \text{vessels, } m, \rho_E, \theta_E \xrightarrow{} v_{\text{eff}}
\;}
\end{equation*}

\section{Headline result of Model~2}

\begin{quote}
\emph{Three ingredients --- continuous EMT, ECM fiber + integrin
contact guidance, hypoxia + dynamic vasculature --- together convert
the cohesive immune-excluded tumor of Model~1 into a realistically
invasive phenotype: a hypoxic mesenchymal core drives MMP secretion
that degrades local ECM, mesenchymal cells lose cadherin and stream
outward along the resulting fiber tracks. Adding CAFs to the
microenvironment supplies the missing \emph{structural} mechanism
behind the "immune-excluded" phenotype --- the desmoplastic ECM ring
mechanically blocks T-cell access regardless of chemotaxis strength.
Hypoxia couples to immunity through reduced kill probability and
boosted suppressant secretion in low-$O_2$ regions, making hypoxic
cores resistance reservoirs that explain why anti-PD-1 monotherapy
fails on cold tumors with high hypoxic fraction.}
\end{quote}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Comparison: Model~1 vs.\ Model~2}

\noindent
Model~1 demonstrates the \emph{immunological} core: three phenotypes
from minimal rules, combination-therapy logic from active-matter
dynamics. It is sufficient to answer the question "what is the simplest
physical system that gives the hot / excluded / cold trichotomy?"

\noindent
Model~2 adds the \emph{mechanobiology} layer: spatial heterogeneity of
matrix and oxygen, cell-state plasticity, force-sensitive adhesion.
These are necessary to ask quantitative questions about invasion speed,
stromal stiffness response, hypoxic-fraction-stratified therapy
prediction, and TACS-3-correlated outcomes.

\subsection{What's testable}

The model makes several predictions amenable to experimental falsification:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Sharp $\rho_I$ threshold for anti-PD-1 response}
        (Model~1). Above a $\rho_I$ threshold, anti-PD-1 alone is
        sufficient; below, combination therapy is needed. Matches
        Tumeh et al.\ (2014) baseline-TIL-density data.
  \item \textbf{Timing of combo therapy matters.} If TAM
        repolarization fires \emph{after} significant $c_{IL10}$ has
        built, the M1-bias is fought by self-reinforcing feedback and
        may fail. Early combo is more
        effective than sequential.
  \item \textbf{Non-monotone invasion in $\rho_E$} (Model~2,
        Section~\ref{sec:m2-integrins}). Stromal density should have an
        optimum.
  \item \textbf{Hypoxic-fraction-dependent combo failure} (Model~2,
        Section~\ref{sec:m2-hypoxia}). Tumors with high HIF should be
        relatively combo-resistant.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}

The model is 2D, periodic, and minimal. Model~2 \emph{does} capture
vasculature dynamically (the \texttt{sim\_tme.py} angiogenesis loop) and
adds NK, DC, and MDSC species, but it still abstracts away: lymph-node
trafficking, adaptive antigen heterogeneity and the long-timescale
immunoediting it produces, dendritic-cell priming as a true
adaptive-immunity loop (we treat it as a local recruit term), clonal
evolution, and 3D tumor geometry. The full nematic-tensor remodeling
of $\mathbf{Q}$ is implemented but currently disabled in production
runs to keep parameter count manageable. The phase diagram remains the
"useful idealization" sweet spot --- minimal physics that still
generates the clinical trichotomy in Model~1, with Model~2 layered on
to ask quantitative invasion / mechanobiology questions.

\section*{Biology reference}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Biology reference}
\label{sec:bioref}
%==========================================================================

A glossary of the biology jargon used above, for the computational
physicist who built this model without a background in tumor immunology.

\paragraph{Cadherin.}
A family of transmembrane proteins that mediate calcium-dependent
cell-cell adhesion. E-cadherin is the canonical epithelial marker;
its loss is a hallmark of EMT. Modeled as the attractive shoulder of
$\mathbf{F}_{cc}$, Section~\ref{sec:m1-extensions}. \emph{Friedl \& Gilmour, Nat
Cell Biol 2012.}

\paragraph{CAF (Cancer-Associated Fibroblast).}
Stromal myofibroblasts that secrete ECM (collagen, fibronectin), MMPs,
TGF-$\beta$, and CXCL12. They form the desmoplastic stroma of
immune-excluded tumors. Not in Model~1 or 2; see
\texttt{docs/extensions.md}, Part C. \emph{Kalluri, Nat Rev Cancer 2016.}

\paragraph{CD8$^+$ T cell.}
The cytotoxic effector lymphocyte. Recognizes tumor antigen presented
on MHC-I, kills via perforin/granzyme on contact. The "T cell" species
in our model. \emph{Kaech \& Cui, Nat Rev Immunol 2012.}

\paragraph{Checkpoint inhibitor.}
A class of immunotherapies (anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4) that
block inhibitory receptors on T cells, "releasing the brakes" on
anti-tumor immunity. Mechanistically equivalent in our model to lowering
$\alpha$. \emph{Pardoll, Nat Rev Cancer 2012.}

\paragraph{CSF1R inhibitor.}
A drug targeting the colony-stimulating-factor-1 receptor on
macrophages, depleting M2-skewed TAMs or repolarizing them toward M1.
Maps onto raising $b_{M_1}$. \emph{Cassier et al., Lancet Oncol 2024.}

\paragraph{CXCL9, CXCL10.}
Chemokines secreted by tumor-infiltrating M1 macrophages, dendritic
cells, and IFN-$\gamma$-stimulated tumor cells that attract CD8$^+$ T
cells via CXCR3. Modeled as $c_a$. \emph{Tokunaga et al., Cancer Treat
Rev 2018.}

\paragraph{CXCL12 (SDF-1).}
Chemokine secreted by CAFs that retains T cells in the peritumoral
stroma. Drives the "excluded" phenotype mechanistically. Not in
Model~1/2. \emph{Feig et al., PNAS 2013.}

\paragraph{EMT (epithelial-mesenchymal transition).}
A cell-state transition in which epithelial cells lose cadherin-mediated
adhesion and apical-basal polarity, and gain motility, MMP secretion,
and invasive behavior. Reversible. Often partial (hybrid E/M state).
\emph{Lu, Jolly, Levine, Onuchic, PNAS 2013. Pastushenko \& Blanpain,
Trends Cell Biol 2019.}

\paragraph{HIF (hypoxia-inducible factor).}
A transcription factor stabilized under low oxygen; it activates a
$\gtrsim$100-gene program including VEGF, glycolysis, EMT, and
immunosuppression. The cellular sensor in our hypoxia extension.
\emph{Semenza, Cell 2014.}

\paragraph{Hot / cold / excluded tumor.}
Clinical immunology classification by CD8$^+$ T-cell distribution.
\emph{Hot}: T cells inside the tumor mass (good prognosis,
checkpoint-responsive). \emph{Cold}: few or no T cells anywhere
(bad prognosis, checkpoint-unresponsive). \emph{Excluded}: T cells form
a ring around the tumor but do not infiltrate the core
(intermediate prognosis, combo therapy needed). Mapped to our three
phases. \emph{Galon \& Bruni, Nat Rev Drug Discov 2019.}

\paragraph{IFN-$\gamma$ (interferon-$\gamma$).}
A pro-inflammatory cytokine secreted by activated T and NK cells.
Drives macrophages toward M1, upregulates MHC-I on tumor cells,
recruits more T cells. Implicitly bundled into the $b_{M_1}$ term in
the polarization equation.

\paragraph{IL-10 (interleukin-10).}
An immunosuppressive cytokine secreted by M2 macrophages, Tregs, and
some tumor cells. Inhibits T-cell activation and effector function.
Modeled explicitly as $c_{IL10}$. \emph{Saraiva \& O'Garra, Nat Rev
Immunol 2010.}

\paragraph{Immunoediting.}
The three-phase model of host-tumor co-evolution (elimination,
equilibrium, escape) over years; broadly parallel to our three phases
but on a much longer timescale and with adaptive mutation as the
driver. \emph{Dunn, Old, Schreiber, Annu Rev Immunol 2004.}

\paragraph{Integrin.}
A heterodimeric transmembrane receptor that binds ECM ligands
(fibronectin, collagen, laminin) and transmits force across the
membrane. Mediator of cell-ECM adhesion and mechanotransduction.
Modeled as $e_i$ in Section~\ref{sec:m2-integrins}.
\emph{Hynes, Cell 2002.}

\paragraph{M1 / M2 macrophage polarization.}
Macrophages are functionally plastic. M1 (IFN-$\gamma$/LPS driven,
pro-inflammatory, phagocytic, TNF/IL-12 producing) and M2
(IL-4/IL-10/TGF-$\beta$ driven, anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive,
VEGF producing) are the two endpoints of a continuum. Tumor-associated
macrophages (TAMs) are typically M2-skewed. \emph{Mantovani et al.,
Nat Rev Clin Oncol 2017.}

\paragraph{MHC-I (major histocompatibility complex class I).}
Cell-surface protein that presents intracellular peptide fragments
(including tumor neoantigens) for inspection by CD8$^+$ T cells.
Downregulated in some tumors to evade T-cell killing; loss-of-MHC-I
sensitizes the cell to NK killing instead. Implicit in our $p_{\text{kill}}$
parameter --- low-MHC-I tumors have low effective $p_{\text{kill}}$.

\paragraph{MMP (matrix metalloproteinase).}
Zinc-dependent proteases (MMP-1, -2, -9, MT1-MMP, etc.) that degrade
ECM components. Secreted by tumor cells, CAFs, and macrophages.
Modeled as the field $m$ in Section~\ref{sec:m1-extensions}.
\emph{Page-McCaw, Ewald, Werb, Nat Rev Mol Cell Biol 2007.}

\paragraph{NK (natural killer) cell.}
An innate lymphocyte that kills cells with low MHC-I ("missing self")
or stress-ligand expression. Not modeled in our system; relevant to
escape from $\alpha$-driven CD8 exclusion. \emph{Vivier et al.,
Science 2011.}

\paragraph{Phagocytosis.}
Engulfment of a target cell by a phagocyte (M1 macrophage, neutrophil,
or dendritic cell). In our model, the M1 macrophage rule of
Section~\ref{sec:m1-macrophage}. \emph{Feng et al., Annu Rev Immunol 2019.}

\paragraph{PD-1 / PD-L1 / CTLA-4.}
The three most clinically important T-cell inhibitory receptor systems.
PD-1 (on T cells) ligated by PD-L1 (on tumor and immune cells)
suppresses T-cell killing; CTLA-4 (on T cells) competes with the
co-stimulatory CD28 receptor. Blocking these is the
"checkpoint-inhibitor" class of immunotherapy. \emph{Topalian, Drake,
Pardoll, Cancer Cell 2015.}

\paragraph{TACS-3 (tumor-associated collagen signature 3).}
A histologic signature: collagen fibers aligned radially around the
tumor margin. Predicts invasion. The phenomenon Model~2's nematic
director field captures. \emph{Provenzano et al., BMC Med 2006.}

\paragraph{TGF-$\beta$ (transforming growth factor-$\beta$).}
A multifunctional cytokine. In the tumor microenvironment it drives
EMT, suppresses T cells, induces Tregs, and stimulates CAF and ECM
deposition. Implicit in our $c_s$ field (and bundled with IL-10 in
$c_{IL10}$). \emph{Mariathasan et al., Nature 2018.}

\paragraph{TIL (tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte).}
Lymphocytes (mostly T cells) found inside or at the margin of a tumor.
TIL count and spatial distribution are prognostic and predictive of
immunotherapy response. \emph{Galon et al., Science 2006.}

\paragraph{Treg (regulatory T cell).}
CD4$^+$ CD25$^+$ FOXP3$^+$ T-cell subset that suppresses effector T-cell
function. Recruited to tumors via CCL22. Not modeled in our system;
their suppressive effect is bundled into $c_s$.
\emph{Tanaka \& Sakaguchi, Cell Death Dis 2025.}

\paragraph{Vasculature / VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor).}
The blood-vessel network that delivers oxygen and removes waste.
Tumor cells under hypoxia secrete VEGF, driving abnormal angiogenesis.
Our Model~2 oxygen field is the abstraction of vascular delivery.
\emph{Carmeliet \& Jain, Nature 2011.}
